Perceptual Illusions

Perceptual Illusions contain conflicting data that cause you to perceive the image in a way that differs from reality. However, an illusion is not always a misinterpretation, but rather it convinces us that the real-life version of an object is false. Through the experience of basic interaction with objects, the brain builds perceptual categories of objects. The perceptual categories then serve as templates where perception occurs through the process of matching sensory input patterns to perceptual templates. The matching process is what gives us the experience of ground relationships. A way to test the reliability of these perceptual templates is through perceptual illusions. They provide a way to experience the template matching process first hand.

Cognitive Illusions

Cognitive illusions (one of three types of illusions; literal, physiological, cognitive) can be defined as unconscious interferences with an object from the viewer's knowledge and assumptions about the world. There are three types of cognitive illusions:

  1. Ambiguous
  2. Distorting
  3. Paradox

Ambiguous Illusions

Allows the viewer to have two valid interpretations of the object. They can visualise both interpretations at different times. However, both cannot be seen at the same time as it would interfere with the full perception of either one, and the brain simply does not allow that.

You can experience the cube in two distinct ways: 1.With the yellow face being the front of the cube, facing in the top left direction. 2.With the yellow face being the back of the cube, with the rest of the cube coming out in the right direction. You can change one orientation to the other by simply thinking about it, however you cannot ‘hold’ one orientation for very long. The first interpretation the Brain makes which is incorrect is that the shape is 3D, although we consciously know that the cube is a bunch of flat lines on a screen. These lines could potentially be interpreted as something else other than a cube. However, the brain sees the cube as the most likely interpretations. The brain sees both orientations as equally likely. Disruptions of any sort (eg. blinking, eye-movements, top-down commands) causes the orientations to ‘flip’.

Distorting Illusions

These illusions are characterized by distortions of size, length and curvature through exploitation of the early visual system

This illusion consists of individual rows which have been displaced. The grey lines seem as if they are at an angle with respect to one another- in fact they all parallel. To explain this using the hypothesis that the mind is made up of modules in which their processes are not accessible to the conscious awareness of a person, a person is still able to be consciously aware that of the illusion that is taking place but will nevertheless experience the illusion. This shows that the visual system is not cognitively impenetrable and counts against the claim that perceptual states are belief-like (ie.you see what you believe).

Paradox Illusions

Generated by objects that are paradoxical in real life. Such illusions are dependent on a cognitive misunderstanding that adjacent edges must join.

When looking at the Penrose triangle you will see something that appears physically possible, but after looking at it for some time, you can see that it is physically impossible to construct such a shape. However, your perception of the triangle does not change for the same reason your perception of the Cafe Wall illusion does not change after recognising how the illusion works, using the hypothesis that the mind is made up of modules in which the processes are not accessible to the conscious awareness of a person. Due to this, a person will still experience the illusion of the Penrose Triangle although they are consciously aware that the illusion is taking place. This shows that the visual system is not cognitively impenetrable and counts against the claim that perceptual states are belief-like (ie.you see what you believe).

Checkerboard Illusion

The checkerboard illusion is an example of colour illusion.
The squares A and B on the image on the right are the same colour.
Don't believe me? Drag the rectangle box onto the image for proof.
The brain makes a few assumptions here which allow the illusion to take place:

  1. That a checkerboard is formed of contrasting squares.

  2. Shadows often have soft edges whilst paint boundaries are sharp. So, the visual system tends to ignore gradual changes in light level so it can determine the colour of the surface where the shadow is without being misled.

  3. The Brain sees the images as being lit by a light source coming from the right. That light source casts a shadow on the checkerboard, supposedly making all the squares that lie in the shadow’s oath darker, but not dark enough that the light and dark colours and indistinguishable.

So why is understanding how illusions work important?

Neuroscientists do research into visual illusions as they help to explain how the human Brain processes visual stimuli. If an illusion can capture attention in this way, then this suggests that the brain processes these visual clues rapidly and unconsciously. This also suggests that perhaps optical illusions represent what our brains like to see or what they ignore. For example, in Troxler's Fading (see right) if you stare at the centre point, the coloured circles surrounding 'dissapears' and fade into the background. This shows that when the Brain is confronted with the same boring stimuli, for an extended period of time, it will maximise it's efficiency by ignoring it and use those brain cycles for something else.
